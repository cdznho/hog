Quantum

Medicine's hardest problems are not clinical. They are computational. A conversation that should give the optimists something real to hold onto.

K
Dr. Kris Naudts  ·  Firgun Ventures Dispatch  ·  Quantum & Life Sciences

There is a scene that John Morton, director of the UK's Quantum Biomedicine Hub, describes almost as a comedy sketch. Early days of a quantum sensing project. A physicist, a biochemist, and a clinician from Great Ormond Street Hospital are gathered around a coffee table. All three keep using the word sensitivity. All three mean something entirely different. The physicist is speaking in femtotesla per root hertz. The biochemist in nanomolar concentrations. The clinician — more practically, and perhaps more wisely — simply wants to know how much blood she would need to take from a sick child to produce a measurable signal.

Three people. One word. Three worlds.

What makes the story encouraging rather than dispiriting is that they sorted it out. The translation happened. And if the broader ambition of quantum medicine rests on anything, it rests on that kind of slow, painstaking work of building a common language where none previously existed.

The Hospital and the Qubit

It still sounds faintly absurd: a hospital embracing quantum computing. The Cleveland Clinic — enormous, storied, synonymous with the best of American cardiology — has done exactly that, in partnership with IBM. Its counterpart in ambition, the Mayo Clinic, has gone the same direction via Google and Sandbox AQ. Whether this is genuine scientific conviction or an acute institutional desire to be on the right side of history is, at this stage, probably both. It does not much matter. The infrastructures are being built.

Across the Atlantic, the UK has taken its characteristic route: public money, long timelines, and a deliberate focus. The Quantum Biomedicine Hub at UCL has £24 million over five years and one singular mandate — healthcare. Not computing in the abstract. Not cryptography. Not even quantum sensing in general. Healthcare. Clinicians were brought into the programme from the outset, not as grateful recipients of technology dreamed up elsewhere, but as co-authors of the problem statement.

The typical physicist just carries on making the sensor more and more sensitive — and doesn't necessarily know where to stop.

John Morton, Quantum Biomedicine Hub, UCL

That observation is not a reproach. It is, in its way, a tribute to scientific temperament. But it explains everything about why the clinical partnership matters. A sensor of extraordinary precision is worthless in a GP surgery if it requires coolant systems that belong in a physics department. The Hub's first task is not to build the most impressive instrument. It is to define what good enough actually looks like — and build toward that.

The Diamond in the Machine

Diamonds, as it turns out, are not forever. Or rather: they are more useful than their permanence suggests.

Naturally occurring diamond is impure. There are vacancies in the carbon lattice, nitrogen atoms where carbon atoms ought to be. Quantum researchers have found ways to isolate the signal from these individual atomic defects — nitrogen-vacancy centres, in the language of the field. And because diamond can be produced in nanoparticle form, at relatively low cost, those nano-diamonds can be coated to seek out specific biomarkers in a biological sample.

The result is diagnostics of a sensitivity that makes the lateral flow tests of the Covid era look primitive. The Hub has demonstrated improvements of over 100,000-fold. Enough to detect a single copy of the HIV virus. That same platform can, in principle, be adapted for Parkinson's biomarkers, tuberculosis, any number of conditions where early and low-resource detection changes the outcome. Morton talks of these sensors working in GP surgeries, in shopping centres, in rural clinics. The ambition is to have them in NHS trusts by 2030.

One wants to be careful here. These are lab results, not deployed technologies. The distance between a proof of concept and a device in a Scottish rural clinic is long and expensive and strewn with regulatory hurdles. But the science is real. That is already more than can be said for a great deal of what circulates as health innovation.

The Longer Game

Quantum sensing is the near-term story. The longer and more transformative one involves computing, and specifically its capacity to simulate molecular chemistry at a level classical machines cannot approach.

Consider cytochrome P450. It governs how the liver processes most drugs. Its behaviour is quantum mechanical in a way that scales exponentially in computational complexity. No classical computer can model it faithfully. A quantum computer, eventually, can. The implications for drug discovery — and for the human cost of the decade-long process that currently separates a molecule from a medicine — are significant enough that China has reportedly set its sights on compressing that timeline from years to hours.

On the Question of When

Prediction markets put commercially meaningful quantum advantage somewhere between 2033 and 2035. DARPA's quantum benchmark programme — which asks not merely whether a useful quantum computer can be built, but whether one can be built at a cost that justifies the value — has set its own deadline at 2033. The consistency is at least suggestive.

Morton's two companies sit at either end of the bridge: Quantum Motion building scalable silicon-based hardware toward the million-qubit threshold; Spacecraft making today's algorithms efficient enough to extract more from the machines that already exist.

A million qubits. The number sounds totemic. Morton is careful to explain that the challenge is not just quantity but quality — physical qubits must be grouped into logical ones capable of running the kinds of calculations that matter. And the machines themselves must reach a form factor and a cost that allows many of them to be deployed in many places. Including, one day, places like the Cleveland Clinic.

The Startup Map and Its Discontents

The Tony Blair Institute counts roughly 500 quantum startups worldwide. About 150 in the United States, unsurprisingly. Sixty-five in the United Kingdom — second globally, which is either impressive or sobering depending on how one views the gap. The majority, Morton observes, sit in the computing and cryptography part of the ecosystem. Sensing startups are fewer and earlier.

The reason is not scientific. It is commercial. Diagnostics have never been the most compelling investment proposition. It is considerably easier to raise money for the remedy than for the test that finds the problem in the first place. One can argue the moral case for early detection until the room empties. The economics run the other way.

That tension will not disappear. But it may shift as clinical validation accumulates and as the platform nature of these technologies becomes clearer to investors not yet fluent in femtoteslas. A sensor that can be adapted across dozens of disease areas is an infrastructure play, not a single-product bet. That framing tends to travel better in venture capital circles.

The Thing Nobody Wants to Talk About

Quantum technologies are dual-use. A sensor developed to detect Parkinson's biomarkers can, in principle, be adapted for navigation, surveillance, or targeting. A computer powerful enough to simulate drug molecules is powerful enough to break most of the encryption that currently protects global communications. The RSA alarm — the 1990s realisation that quantum computing threatened public-key cryptography — was, Morton notes, precisely what lit the funding fuse for the entire modern quantum programme. Threat became investment became tool.

He is careful, though. The risk of over-emphasising security applications is that it produces classification, restriction, and the closing of the international collaborations that the field genuinely needs to progress. The best quantum physicists are not all American. Insisting on treating the technology primarily as a weapon is a good way to build a slower, worse weapon.

The coverage, as Dr. Kris Naudts observes in this conversation, skews alarmist. Decryption. Military advantage. The end of secure communications as we know them. What gets less airtime is the possibility that the same technology could tell a doctor in rural Uganda whether her patient has tuberculosis, from a blood sample smaller than a teardrop, in the time it takes to make a cup of tea.

A Note from the Frontier

It is fashionable, in certain quarters of the health world, to be sceptical of technology's promises. The scepticism is not without foundation. Silicon Valley has not, on balance, made medicine more humane. AI has been five years away from transforming diagnostics for about fifteen years. The gap between a TED talk and a clinical trial is a canyon.

And yet. The science in this conversation is not speculative. The nano-diamond results are published. The IBM partnership is operational. The clinical engagement at UCL is a structural feature of the programme, not a press release. One does not have to be naïve to notice that something real is being built here — incrementally, unglamorously, by people who understand both the physics and the clinic, and who have taken the time to learn each other's language.

That, more than any qubit count, is the thing worth watching.

It is heartwarming what quantum could do and how it could help the field forward — and us as humans.
