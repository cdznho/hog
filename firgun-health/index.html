The Body at the
Quantum Level

A conversation with the Cleveland Clinic's quantum pioneer and the UK's Quantum Biomedicine Hub director reveals how qubits, diamond sensors, and a good deal of interdisciplinary patience may soon transform how we diagnose, treat, and understand disease.

F
Firgun Ventures Dispatch  ·  Quantum & Life Sciences

There is a moment in this podcast conversation that crystallises the whole challenge and promise of quantum medicine. John Morton, director of the UK's Quantum Biomedicine Hub at University College London, describes the first planning meeting for a quantum sensing project. Around the coffee table sat a physicist, a biochemist, and a clinician from Great Ormond Street Hospital. All three kept using the word sensitivity. All three meant something completely different.

The physicist was speaking in femtotesla per root hertz — a measure of magnetic field resolution. The biochemist talked in nanomolar and picomolar concentrations. The clinician simply wanted to know: how much blood would she need to take from a sick child to get a measurable signal?

That anecdote is funny, humbling, and ultimately hopeful. Because they figured it out. And that capacity for translation — between the quantum world and the clinical one — is exactly what the field is now building at scale.

Why Hospitals Are Betting on Quantum

The Cleveland Clinic sits at the vanguard of this shift in the United States. Renowned as a global leader in cardiology and cardiac surgery and home to one of the largest academic medical centres in the world, the clinic made an early and deliberate move into quantum computing, partnering with IBM. The question of why a hospital would do this is the right one to ask. The honest answer sits somewhere between genuine scientific curiosity and the recognition that the most transformative clinical problems — drug discovery, molecular simulation, personalised medicine at scale — are precisely the kind of problems that classical computers cannot solve.

Across the Atlantic, the UK has taken a parallel route through the quantum sensing door. The Quantum Biomedicine Hub, funded with £24 million over five years and embedded in a broader national quantum strategy, has made healthcare its singular focus. Where most quantum programmes cast wide across computing, communication, sensing, and cryptography, the Hub narrowed the aperture deliberately. Clinicians sit inside the programme from day one — not as recipients of the technology, but as co-designers.

The typical physicist just carries on making the sensor more and more sensitive — and doesn't necessarily know where to stop.

John Morton, Quantum Biomedicine Hub, UCL

That quote is not a criticism. It is a precise description of why embedding clinical context matters so much. A sensor that is ten times more sensitive than anything that currently exists is useless in a GP surgery if it still requires a liquid-helium coolant system the size of a wardrobe. The Hub's job is to define the right target specifications — and then build toward them.

Diamonds, Diamonds, Diamonds

If quantum computing grabs the headlines, quantum sensing is where the near-term clinical impact is most tangible. And nothing in the sensing world is more striking right now than what can be done with a diamond.

Naturally occurring diamond is not perfectly pure carbon. It contains atomic defects — nitrogen atoms, vacancies in the crystal lattice — and researchers have learned to isolate and manipulate the quantum signals that come from those individual atomic imperfections. These nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centres turn out to be extraordinary sensors.

The practical magic is this: diamond can be manufactured in nanoparticle form, at relatively low cost. Those nano-diamonds can be functionalised — essentially coated — to bind to specific biomarkers. Attach the right surface chemistry, and you have a sensor that will seek out a target molecule and report back with quantum precision. The Hub has shown it is possible to improve diagnostic sensitivity by over 100,000-fold compared to a standard lateral flow test — enough to detect a single copy of the HIV virus.

This is a platform technology. The same nano-diamond sensing approach, once validated for HIV, can be adapted for COVID antigens, Parkinson's biomarkers, tuberculosis, and a long list of conditions where early, accurate, low-resource detection could change outcomes dramatically. Morton envisions these sensors working not just in hospital labs but in GP surgeries, shopping centres, and rural clinics — the kind of democratised diagnostics that could reshape global health equity.

The Drug Discovery Horizon

The more distant, more transformative prize sits in quantum computing's ability to simulate molecular chemistry. Classical computers cannot faithfully model the quantum behaviour of complex molecules. This is not a hardware limitation that will be resolved by Moore's Law — it is a fundamental constraint. A molecule like cytochrome P450, which governs how the liver metabolises most drugs, involves quantum interactions that scale exponentially in computational complexity. Simulating it accurately requires a quantum computer.

China has reportedly set a goal of compressing the drug discovery process from years to hours using quantum and AI. The current best estimate in prediction markets is that commercially meaningful quantum advantage will arrive somewhere between 2033 and 2035 — a window that sits neatly around DARPA's own quantum benchmark deadline of 2033. The programme asks not merely whether a quantum computer can be built, but whether one can be built where the cost justifies the value. That is the right question.

The Qubit Challenge

Today's quantum computers are early prototypes. Reaching the applications most likely to transform medicine — drug discovery, protein folding, complex biological simulation — requires machines at the scale of hundreds of thousands to millions of physical qubits.

John Morton's company, Quantum Motion, is working to build those machines using the same silicon transistor technology that powers every smartphone on earth. Spacecraft, his second company, focuses on making quantum algorithms more efficient — squeezing more out of today's hardware while waiting for the hardware itself to mature.

The Startup Landscape

According to the Tony Blair Institute, approximately 500 quantum startups now exist worldwide. Around 150 are in the United States. The United Kingdom, perhaps counterintuitively, is second globally with roughly 65 companies. That cluster reflects deliberate policy: a decade-long national quantum programme, strong university research infrastructure, and an investment landscape that has been, by Morton's account, genuinely excellent.

The interesting tension in this landscape is that the majority of quantum startups are still in computing and cryptography. Sensing startups are earlier and fewer — in part because quantum sensing companies face a genuine commercial challenge. Diagnostics, however revolutionary, have historically been less attractive to investors than therapeutics. The principle is an uncomfortable one: it is easier to fund the remedy than the test that finds the problem.

That may change. As the sensing technologies mature and the clinical validation builds, the commercial logic should shift. A platform technology that can detect single molecules, work in any setting, and be adapted across dozens of disease areas is precisely the kind of horizontal infrastructure that venture capital finds compelling once the proof points exist.

What the Medical System Needs to Catch Up

One tension that runs quietly through this conversation is the skills gap. Medical education is not software-oriented. It is emphatically not quantum-oriented. Bringing quantum tools into clinical settings requires a new kind of bilingualism — or rather, trilingualism — among physicists, biochemists, and clinicians.

The Hub's approach has been to bring clinicians in from the beginning, learning their rhythms, attending meetings they already attend rather than asking overstretched doctors to come to new workshops. That clinical engagement is not a soft addendum to the science. It is the methodology.

The early findings suggest that clinicians, once in dialogue, are enthusiastic. The technology is not threatening to them — it is hopeful. And that matters. The receptiveness of the clinical community is not guaranteed in any technological transition, but in quantum health it seems to be real.

A Note on Geopolitics

The conversation closes on two themes that loom over all of this: China and defence. On the former, the honest answer is that the most advanced publicly accessible quantum systems remain predominantly in the United States, but what is happening behind closed doors in Chinese laboratories is genuinely difficult to know. Following the science, reading the papers — that is possible. Knowing what is not published is, by definition, harder.

On defence, Morton offers a measured and thoughtful position. Quantum sensors and quantum computers are general-purpose technologies. A sensor developed for cardiac imaging can, in principle, be adapted for navigation or detection. That dual-use nature is real. But over-emphasising security applications risks undermining the international collaboration that the field needs to actually progress. The RSA encryption alarm — the recognition in the 1990s that quantum computers could break public-key cryptography — is what lit the funding fuse for the entire modern quantum programme. The threat created the investment that built the tools that will now, eventually, diagnose the diseases.

There is a circularity to that which feels appropriate for a field that operates, in some sense, at the level of loops and superpositions.

What We're Watching at Firgun

The intersection of quantum and health sits at an early but inflecting stage. The timelines are honest — commercially transformative quantum computing remains a decade away on most credible estimates. But quantum sensing is genuinely closer. The diagnostics applications, particularly diamond-based biosensors, represent a real near-term opportunity where the science is proven at lab scale and the clinical translation is underway.

For investors, the signal is not to wait for the computing revolution. It is to pay attention to the sensing companies and the clinical translation infrastructure being built now. The hub model — tightly coupling physicists, engineers, and clinicians around sector-specific applications — is the organisational innovation that will determine which technologies actually reach patients.

That, after all, is the only benchmark that ultimately matters.

It is heartwarming what quantum could do and how it could help the field forward — and us as humans.
